Capacity Modeling · Lesson 1

Does capacity add up, or does the weakest link win?

The single decision behind modelling a person, a person + a resource, several tracks, or a whole department: do the sources sum, or is one of them a bottleneck?

You already model a single Subject's capacity well (Base Capacity, Reducers, one Declared Unit). The moment a Subject has more than one capacity source — a person plus a CI pipeline, two coupled tracks, five departments — you face one fork, and getting it wrong makes the heatmap confidently lie.

The fork

Sum

C = c₁ + c₂ + …

Sources are parallel and interchangeable. Any of them can take the work. Three cross-trained people get through three people's worth.

Min (bottleneck)

C = min(c₁, c₂, …)

Sources are coupled in series — all needed to produce output. The slowest one sets the pace. This is Goldratt's constraint: “no chain is stronger than its weakest link.”1

The one-question test: “If I add one more unit of this source, does total output rise?” If yes for every source → sum. If only one source moves the needle → that's the constraint, use min, and adding anywhere else is wasted.2

Why it bites: the composite case

Take your “person + infra” example. One engineer who can prepare 20 deployments a day, backed by a CI pipeline that can only run 8 builds a day. Naively summing (20 + 8 = 28) is nonsense — they measure different steps of one pipeline. The real throughput is min(20, 8) = 8. Hire a second engineer and it's still 8: you added to a non-constraint. That is the entire point of the Theory of Constraints.3

Your rollup = summation decision (ADR-0002) is exactly the sum rule — and it's correct only for fungible sources. Coupled tracks (person + infra, multi-stage departments) need min. That's a genuine gap in the current model: a candidate ADR-0004 ("composite capacity: sum for fungible, min for coupled") and possibly a per-Subject "composition" setting.
