Capacity Modeling · Lesson 4

From raw to effective capacity

The number you declare is rarely the room you actually have. Holidays, meetings and part-time patterns eat into it. This lesson turns raw declared capacity into the real Reference that Subtractive mode paints against.

Say you tell everyone, "I can do 8 hours a day." That's the number on paper. But real life takes bites out of it: a team meeting, being off on Fridays, a public holiday. So the room you actually have is smaller than the number you said.

Meetings and admin quietly ate the top two hours. Same person, smaller real room.

And some days are totally closed — a public holiday, say. A closed day isn't a "busy" day and it isn't an "empty" day; it's just off. We paint it grey, not red or blue, so you can tell "nothing happened here" apart from "this day was quiet".

quietslammedgrey = closed day
The room you really have is smaller than the number you declared. A fully closed day (like a holiday) is grey, not coloured.

In Subtractive mode you declare a Base Capacity: the room per day before anything eats into it, in your Declared Unit. It can be flat, or follow a weekday pattern (e.g. weekdays 450, weekends 0).

Real life is modelled with Reducers. A Reducer is a multiplier in [0, 1] applied over a span: a holiday is 0, reduced staffing might be 0.6. When several overlap they stack multiplicatively, and the day's Effective Capacity is:

Effective Capacity = Base Capacity × product of all active Reducers.
This is the Reference — the value that maps to full-red 1.0 — for that Cell in Subtractive mode.

Worked calc. Base 100, with two active Reducers 0.8 (a training afternoon) and 0.5 (half the team out):

StepCalculationResult
Base Capacitydeclared per-day amount100
Apply reducer 1100 × 0.880
Apply reducer 280 × 0.540
Effective Capacity100 × 0.8 × 0.540

So a Consumer load of 40 on that day reads as full red (1.0), even though the paper number was 100. When a Reducer (or the product of several) drives Effective Capacity to 0, that day is a Blackout: rendered neutral grey, not red. Load that lands on a Blackout is kept grey but flagged — a warning that you've scheduled work onto a closed day.

0.01.0 fullgrey = Blackout · ▲ = load on a closed day

The second week runs hotter because a 0.6 staffing Reducer shrank Effective Capacity — the same load fills more of a smaller room.

This is shrinkage (or the focus factor) from workforce and agile planning: declared time is never fully available, so you subtract non-project time — leave, ceremonies, context overhead — before comparing load to capacity. Reducers are the tool's general form of that subtraction, and they generalise past people to any Subject (a machine's maintenance windows, a kitchen's prep hours).1

Crucially, "full" should not mean 100% of raw declared capacity. Sustainable utilisation targets sit around 75–85%, not 100% — the remaining headroom absorbs variability. You can encode part of that headroom directly as a standing Reducer, or handle it at the utilisation-target stage (Lesson 6).2

Why multiply, not add? Reducers compose as independent proportional cuts, so Base × 0.8 × 0.5 keeps the result in [0, Base] and can never go negative. Additive stacking (Base − 20% − 50% − 40%) would drop below zero and produce nonsensical negative capacity. Multiplication also matches the semantics: "half the staff, of whom 80% are focused" really is 0.5 × 0.8 = 0.4, not 1 − 0.5 − 0.2.

0.0≥1.0grey = Effective Capacity 0

Edge — reducer 0 vs Blackout. These are the same event seen from two angles: a full public holiday is modelled as a Reducer of 0 over that day, and the result (Effective Capacity = 0) is what we call a Blackout. A Blackout is therefore not a separate primitive — it's the rendering rule for "any day whose reducer-product zeroed the Base". This is also why improving one Reducer while another sits at 0 changes nothing: 0 × 0.9 = 0. Zero is absorbing.

Where this is a simplification: a single per-day multiplier can't express which hours were lost or whether a reduction was elastic (work deferred, not destroyed). The model treats a 0.5 day as "half the room, gone" — it doesn't know the other half slid to tomorrow. For deferral dynamics you need the queueing view hinted at in Lesson 6, not a Reducer.

Why it matters

If you compare load against the declared number, every heatmap runs cool and lies to you. Effective Capacity is the honest denominator: it already knows about holidays, part-time patterns and reduced-staffing spans, so a red Cell means really overloaded — not "overloaded if nobody ever took a day off".

Your turn — set the Reference

Each situation is in Subtractive mode. Decide how to model it.

A one-off public holiday closes the Subject completely for a single day. How should you model it?

A day already has a holiday Reducer of 0. Someone proposes upgrading a separate "tooling" Reducer on that day from 0.7 to 0.95 to gain capacity. What happens to Effective Capacity?

In the tool: set one Base Capacity (flat or weekday-patterned), then add Reducers as dated spans — holiday 0, reduced-staffing 0.6, and so on. The heatmap recomputes Effective Capacity per Cell and repaints; any 0-capacity day turns grey as a Blackout.
