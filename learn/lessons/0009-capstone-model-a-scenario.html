Capacity Modeling · Lesson 9

Capstone: model a scenario end-to-end

Everything in one build. We'll take a real situation — a small marketing team facing a big launch — and turn it into a single, trustworthy heatmap, one decision at a time.

The running example. A 5-person marketing team runs a 6-week product launch. We'll model it in the Declared Unit hours, and watch the launch weeks heat up.

Meet the team: five people who normally handle the usual marketing work — emails, social posts, the odd meeting. That's their normal week: steady, a bit of blue-to-warm, nothing scary.

Now the big launch arrives. For six weeks the team has extra work on top of the normal stuff. At first it's light (planning and prep), then in the final two weeks it goes big (the launch actually happens). Watch the last weeks turn red:

The picture tells the story instantly: the first four weeks are fine, but weeks 5 and 6 glow red — the team is over-full right when the launch lands. Now you can do something about it before it happens: move work earlier, add help, or push a deadline.

Build the normal week first, add the big project on top, then look for the red. Red weeks are where the plan breaks — and now you can see them coming.

Modelling is a fixed sequence of decisions. Here it is, with real numbers for our team.

  1. Pick the Subject / faceting level. We want the whole team, so we don't facet by person — the five people's capacity and load sum into one team Subject.
  2. Choose the Mode. The team has a known, declared capacity, so Subtractive: Cells start blue and fill toward red, and >1.0 is real overload.
  3. Set the Declared Unit. hours. Every Capacity and Consumer below is in hours; the tool won't convert, so we stay consistent.
  4. Set Base Capacity + weekday pattern. 5 people × 6 focused hours = 30 h/day on weekdays, 0 at weekends.
  5. Apply Reducers / Blackouts. One public holiday → a Blackout (Effective Capacity 0, grey). A 2-day off-site conference → a Reducer of 0.5 over those two days (Effective Capacity 15 h/day).
  6. Add Consumers. A Recurring Consumer for BAU: 12 h every weekday. A Spanning Consumer for the launch across all six weeks, shaped with two Phases: prep (weeks 1–4) at 6 h/day, then live (weeks 5–6) at 22 h/day.
  7. Decide Composition. One capacity Track for the team, so Composition is sum (the five are treated as fungible). We'll revisit this at Expert level.
  8. Read the result & set a utilisation band. Target ~80% (Load Intensity ≈ 0.8) as a healthy band, not 100%.

The arithmetic. Load Intensity = consumed ÷ Effective Capacity.

  • Prep weekday: (12 + 6) ÷ 30 = 0.60 — comfortably inside the band.
  • Live weekday: (12 + 22) ÷ 30 = 1.13 — over 1.0, real overload.
  • Live day during the conference: 34 ÷ 15 = 2.27 — deep red, badly over.

The build is mechanical; the judgement calls are where models go right or wrong.

  • Subtractive vs Additive. We chose Subtractive because a defensible capacity number exists (headcount × focused hours). If that number were fiction, Additive would be more honest: relative "hotness" with no false >1.0 overload claim (Lesson 66).
  • One Spanning Consumer with Phases, or several? Modelling the launch as a single Spanning Consumer with prep/live Phases keeps it as one thing you can move or resize. If prep and live are run by different people with independent timelines, splitting into separate Consumers models them more truthfully — at the cost of a busier Scenario.
  • Sum vs min for team capacity. We summed the five as fungible. But if every launch asset needs one legal approver, the team is coupled: real capacity is gated by that approver, so Effective Capacity should be min across Tracks, with the approver as the Constraint (Lesson 77). Summing here would overstate what the team can actually ship.
Where the finished model is still a simplification: collapsing each day to one Load Intensity hides within-day queueing (a day can read 0.9 while work still piles up behind a single approver) and context-switching cost (juggling BAU and launch is more expensive than the raw sum of hours suggests). The heatmap flags which days are hot; it doesn't model the intraday dynamics or the tax of splitting attention.g
