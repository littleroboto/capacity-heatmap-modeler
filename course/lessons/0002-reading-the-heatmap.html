Capacity Modeling · Lesson 2

Reading the heatmap

Lesson 1 said what a heatmap is. This one is about reading a finished one: what each colour is telling you, day by day.

A heatmap is a weather map for busy-ness. Just like a weather map uses colour to show where it's hot and where it's cold, this map uses colour to show which days were quiet and which were slammed. You read it the same way: let your eye find the colours, don't read numbers.

There are three colours to know:

  • Cool blue = a quiet day, lots of room left.
  • Warm red = a busy day, almost full or too full.
  • Grey = a day that was closed — a holiday or day off. Nothing happened, so it isn't hot or cold.
quietslammedgrey = closed

Now point at the map above. Most days are calm blue and sandy. Then look at that run of deep-red squares — that's a busy streak, several slammed days in a row. And spot the single grey square: that day was off, so it stayed colourless.

Blue = quiet. Red = busy. Grey = closed. Grey is not "very busy" — it means nothing was open that day.

Every Cell — one Subject on one day — is painted by its Load Intensity, a single value on one blue→red ramp. Reading the map is reading that value off the colour:

ColourLoad IntensityMeaning
Coolest blue0.0Empty — no load on that day
Blue → sand0.1–0.7Comfortable, room to spare
Sand → red0.8–1.0Near or at the Reference (full)
Deepest red>1.0Overload — past full
Neutral grey—Blackout: Effective Capacity 0 (closed)

Two anchors matter. 0 is empty (coolest). 1.0 is the Reference — the quantity that maps to full red. Anything >1.0 pushes into the deepest red as overload. Grey is different in kind: it isn't a hot value at all, it's a Blackout day whose Effective Capacity is 0.

A worked read. Take this fortnight for one Subject:

0.0 empty1.0+ fullgrey = blackout
  1. The early days sit blue-to-sand: intensities around 0.3–0.6. Comfortable, well under the Reference.
  2. The middle carries a run of deep-red Cells — several days at or above 1.0. That's a sustained overload, not a one-day blip.
  3. One grey Cell sits inside the busy stretch: a Blackout (Effective Capacity 0), read as closed, never as "hottest".

Zoomed out, you rarely stare at single days. A Roll-up View aggregates Load Intensity into weekly or monthly buckets rendered on the same Cells — the grain underneath stays daily, you're just reading a coarser summary of the same values.g

Grey and red answer different questions. Red says how full. Grey says whether the day counted at all. Never average a Blackout into a busy streak.

Load Intensity is a normalised scalar — load ÷ Reference — mapped onto a single perceptual ramp. The design payoff of one ramp is cross-subject comparability: because a person, a kitchen, and a CI pipeline are all painted on the same 0→1+ scale, a red Cell means the same relative thing everywhere, and small multiples become directly scannable side by side.1

The ramp encodes three regimes. Below 1.0: spare room. At 1.0: the Reference is met. Above 1.0: past the Reference — but that top region is only meaningful in Subtractive mode, where the Reference is a real Effective Capacity, so >1.0 is genuine overload. In Additive mode the Reference is just a Normalisation Max, so there is no true "over" (Lesson 3).2 Grey is off the ramp entirely: a Blackout is a categorical state (Effective Capacity 0), deliberately not encoded as an extreme value so a closed day can never be misread as the hottest.

0.0≥1.0 overloadgrey = blackout
Where it's a simplification: a day collapsed to one scalar hides within-day timing and queueing. Two days can both read 0.9 while one flowed smoothly and the other spiked and queued badly at noon. The ramp reports a daily aggregate, not the arrival process underneath — utilisation near a limit says nothing about the variability that actually drives waiting.3

Why reading comes before building

If you can't read a heatmap correctly you can't trust one you build. The two easiest mistakes are treating grey as very hot and treating a single red Cell as a crisis. A crisis is a run of red — a streak — and grey is simply a day that was closed.
