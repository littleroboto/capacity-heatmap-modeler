Capacity Modeling · Lesson 6

Utilisation and the 100% trap

A heatmap that's solid deep-red is a warning light, not a trophy. Being totally full is fragile, not efficient — aim for mostly warm, not scalding.

Think about a motorway. When it's half full, cars flow freely — someone can merge, brake, or speed up and nothing goes wrong. But when it's nearly full, one extra car, one tap of the brakes, and the whole thing seizes into a jam. Same road, wildly different behaviour.

Being "totally full" isn't a win. It means there's no room for surprises. And there are always surprises.

So when a heatmap goes solid deep-red, day after day, that's not a picture of a well-used team — it's the traffic jam waiting to happen:

quietjammed

A healthy heatmap is mostly warm — busy, but with a little cool room left so a bad day doesn't cause a pile-up:

quietslammed
All-red = danger, not success. A good heatmap runs warm with a little slack — like a motorway that still flows.

In Subtractive mode, Load Intensity is utilisation: Load Intensity = consumed ÷ Effective Capacity. So a Cell at 1.0 full-red means demand exactly equals capacity — zero slack. Anything unexpected (a sick day, a late input, a scope bump) has nowhere to go, so it spills into overload (>1.0) or lateness.

Rule of thumb: aim for a sustained Load Intensity of roughly 0.75–0.85, not 1.0. That band keeps a Subject genuinely busy while leaving enough room to absorb normal variability.1

Load IntensityInterpretationWhat to do
0 – 0.5Idle / spare — lots of unused roomTake on more, or reduce Capacity
0.5 – 0.7Comfortable, plenty of slackFine; room to grow
0.7 – 0.85Healthy — the sustainable bandHold here
0.85 – 1.0Tight — little slack, fragileWatch closely; shed load if it persists
> 1.0Overloaded — work spills, lateness buildsCut demand or add Effective Capacity

This is why a deep-red run matters more than a single red Cell. One hot day is noise; a sustained band above 0.85 means the Subject has no buffer left and every wobble now becomes a delay.1

Full-red is not the target. A heatmap parked at 1.0 has optimised away the one thing that keeps it reliable: slack.

The fragility at high utilisation isn't a hunch — it's a queueing result. As utilisation ρ → 1, the expected time work spends waiting grows like 1/(1−ρ): a hockey stick, not a straight line. Going from 80% to 90% utilised doesn't add 10% of wait — it roughly doubles it; 90% to 95% doubles it again.2

Kingman's formula (the VUT equation) makes the drivers explicit: mean wait ≈ Variability × Utilisation × Time. The utilisation term is that same ρ/(1−ρ) blow-up, and it's multiplied by variability — so the more variable the work, the more punishing high utilisation becomes.3

Behind all of it sits Little's Law, the conservation identity: WIP = throughput × cycle time. If throughput is capped by capacity but work keeps arriving, WIP rises — and by Little's Law, so does cycle time. The queue is real even when you can't see it.4

The catch for this tool: a heatmap shows utilisation only — it never shows the queue that utilisation implies. A sustained deep-red run predicts exploding lead times, but the colour is capped, so a Subject pinned at 1.0 for a month looks the same on day 1 and day 30 even though the backlog behind it is compounding.

Where it's a simplification: the daily Load Intensity scalar collapses a whole day to one number, so it cannot render the queue directly. Treat any deep-red run as a leading indicator of blown-out lead times — the heatmap tells you the pressure, not the pile-up it's already producing.

Why this trap is so common

Because "everyone's fully utilised" sounds like good management, and a wall of red looks decisive. Both instincts are backwards. Slack is not waste — it's the capacity to absorb the variability that every real system carries. Optimise it away and the first surprise turns into a jam.
