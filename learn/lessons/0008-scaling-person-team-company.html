Scaling: person → team → company

One model, every level. The same coloured-squares picture works for a single person, a whole team, or the entire company — you just choose how far to zoom out. Nothing new to learn; only what to add together.

You already know the picture: a row of little squares, one per day, coloured by how busy it is. Cool blue means quiet, warm red means slammed. The lovely part is that the same picture works whether you're looking at one person or a thousand.

Want to see each person again? Split it back apart into the row of small pictures above. Rolling up (adding together) and splitting apart are the only two moves. That's how one person, a team, and a company are all the same tool — just zoomed in or out.

Zoom out = add everyone together into one hotter picture. Zoom in = split it back into a small picture per person. Same squares, same colours.

The Subject is not a fixed type — it's a faceting level. Person, team, and company are the same model rolled up to different depths. Two moves control the depth, and both hang off one idea: the Dimension.

A Dimension is a config-declared attribute with a fixed set of allowed values (country, brand, channel, person) that every Capacity and every Consumer is tagged with. Then:

  • Facet by a Dimension → the visual splits into Small Multiples: one heatmap per allowed value.
  • Don't facet → Capacities and Consumers sum across that Dimension (rollup = summation), and the Subject becomes the whole aggregate.

So "person → team → company" is nothing but how far you roll up the person Dimension. Facet by it and each Subject is one person; leave it un-faceted and the Subject is the summed team or company.

The rule: facet by X → you get one heatmap per X. Don't facet by X → everything sums across X into one Subject.

Rollup-by-summation is the tool's default aggregation, and it is correct only for fungible, parallel sources — where the pieces are interchangeable and their capacities genuinely add. Three copy-writers who can each cover any brief are fungible: summing their capacity is right.

For coupled sources it silently lies. If the work must flow through every stage in series (draft → legal sign-off → publish), the aggregate is not the sum of the stages — it's capped by the weakest one. You'd want min, not sum. The heatmap will still happily paint a summed rollup that looks fine while the real throughput is throttled by a single Constraint (see Lesson 77). Aggregation is therefore a modelling decision, not a display toggle: choosing person/team/company depth also implicitly asserts that the things you're summing are additive.

The visual side is Tufte's argument for small multiples: repeating the same graphic across the values of one Dimension makes comparison across that Dimension almost effortless — the eye reads exception and pattern faster than any table.1 Faceting is exactly this device applied to a Dimension.

Where it's a simplification: a Dimension can only be summed if its values share one Declared Unit. If one team's Capacity is in hours and another's is in GBP, the rollup is meaningless — the tool does not convert units, so mixed-unit values can't be summed into one Subject. Facet them instead, or model them as separate Scenarios.g

Why this matters

There is no separate "hierarchy" feature to configure. Because the same machine rolls up by summation, you scale a model just by choosing what to facet by — and you keep the same heatmap reading skills at every level. The one thing to stay honest about: only sum what genuinely adds.
