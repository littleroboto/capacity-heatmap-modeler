Modeling demand: consumers & load shapes

Capacity is the room; demand is what fills it. Here you learn how load gets added to a day — the two kinds of thing that generate it, and how their amounts pile up when they overlap.

A consumer is anything that uses up part of a day — a task, a meeting, a big project. Think of them as blocks you drop into the day.

There are two flavours:

  • Things that happen regularly — like a stand-up meeting every morning.
  • Things that run for a stretch — like a project that lasts a whole month.

When several land on the same day, their blocks stack up. More blocks = a taller pile = a busier, redder day.

Consumers are the things that fill a day. When they overlap, their amounts add up — the day gets taller and redder.

A Consumer is anything that generates load over time. It exists in both Modes. The core rule is simple:

A day's total load = the sum of every active Consumer's amount on that day. "Layering" is just summation.

Consumers come in two shapes:

PatternA per-active-day amount on a recurrence (every weekday, specific weekdays, a cadence), optionally date-boundedA single dated range with a Load Shape

A Load Shape describes how a Spanning Consumer's amount is distributed across its range. A Phase is a named sub-span carrying its own amount — e.g. a prep phase at a low amount, then a live phase at a high amount.

Worked example. A recurring stand-up of 60 min every weekday, plus a spanning campaign with two Phases: prep at 120, then live at 300. The day's load is the sum:

The low steady band is the recurring stand-up; it lifts into prep, then jumps in the live Phase — every Cell is the running sum.

Summing Consumers is the additive-superposition assumption: independent load streams combine linearly, so total demand on a day is Σ amounti. This is the same layering that Additive mode uses to build relative "hotness" with no declared Capacity — here the layered sum is compared against a Reference (Effective Capacity in Subtractive mode), there against a Normalisation Max.1

A Load Shape is a discretised demand curve sampled per day; Phases make it explicitly piecewise-constant (each Phase a step of fixed height). A ramp-up/down is a linear approximation of the same curve. This is deliberately coarse: the grain is one day, so any within-day profile collapses to a single per-day amount.

0.01.0a ramp-up/down Load Shape

Where superposition breaks. Summation assumes streams are independent and additive. Real work has context-switching cost: two half-day tasks on the same day can cost more than one full day, because switching burns setup/attention that neither task "declares". The model can't see that — it will read two 0.5 Consumers as exactly 1.0, never 1.2. Interaction, coordination overhead and queueing (Lesson 6) all live in that gap.2

Where this is a simplification: layering is a linear model of demand. It's exact for fungible, independent work and optimistic whenever tasks interfere. If your real constraint is attention-switching rather than raw hours, a summed heatmap will look cooler than reality — treat a busy multi-Consumer day as a floor, not a ceiling.

Why it matters

Getting demand right is half of every heatmap. Pick the wrong Consumer shape and the picture lies: model a month-long launch as a recurring daily task and you'll smear its ramp flat; model your weekly 1:1s as one big span and you'll bury the rhythm. Match the shape to how the work actually arrives.
