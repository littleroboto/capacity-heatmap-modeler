Capacity Modeling · Lesson 3

Two modes: Subtractive vs Additive

The core fork of the whole tool. Everything about a heatmap is shared except one choice: what sets the point that maps to full red.

There are two ways to decide when a day should look "red hot". Which one you pick depends on a single question: do you know the limit?

Way 1 — you know the limit. Think of a glass that holds 500ml. You know exactly when it's full. Pour in water and the glass goes toward red as it fills; pour past the brim and it's overflowing — truly too much. Red means "near or over the limit you know."

Way 2 — you don't have a fixed limit. Now think about your calendar and the question "which days were my busiest?" There's no hard ceiling — you just want the busy days to stand out from the quiet ones. The busiest day becomes the reddest, and everything else is coloured relative to it. Red just means "hottest compared to the others."

Know the limit? Colour toward that limit (the glass). No fixed limit? Just show which days were hottest relative to each other (the busiest-day view).

The two ways have names. Both paint Cells on the same blue→red ramp; they differ only in what the Reference (full red, 1.0) is.

Subtractive mode — you have a Capacity

The Subject has a declared Capacity. The Reference is the Cell's Effective Capacity, so:

Load Intensity = consumed ÷ effective capacity

A day starts blue and heats up as Consumers use it up. Because the Reference is a real limit, >1.0 is real, meaningful overload.

Worked example. A nurse can see 20 patients/day (Effective Capacity = 20). On a day with 24 booked, Load Intensity = 24 ÷ 20 = 1.2 — deepest red, a genuine 20% overload.

0.0 empty1.0+ overload

Additive mode — no Capacity

The Subject has no declared Capacity. The Reference is a Normalisation Max — the hottest Cell in the visual, or a value you set. Cells start empty and heat up as Consumers layer in, painted relative to that max. There is no true overload: nothing can pass the top of the ramp because the top is the busiest observed Cell.

Worked example. A social account posts on many days with no hard ceiling. The busiest day (say 9 posts) becomes the Normalisation Max and paints deepest red; a 3-post day paints at 3 ÷ 9 = 0.33. It only tells you that day was a third as hot as the busiest — not that anything is "over".

Same machine, one switch. Pick Subtractive when a real limit exists and breaching it matters. Pick Additive when there's no limit and you only want relative heat.

The two modes differ in exactly one thing: what sets the Reference. Subtractive binds the Reference to a declared Effective Capacity; Additive binds it to a Normalisation Max. Everything else — Consumers, the daily grain, summation of load, the single perceptual colour ramp — is shared, unchanged machinery.

That is deliberate deep-module design: one mechanism (normalise load against a Reference, paint on one ramp) exposed through two thin policies for what the Reference is. The narrow interface (a mode flag) hides a large shared implementation, which is exactly the shape a good module should have — a simple surface over substantial, reused internals rather than two parallel code paths.1 It also mirrors the operations-management view that utilisation is always load relative to a reference capacity; the modes just change where that capacity number comes from.2

Edge — switching Additive → Subtractive. A model often starts Additive (no known limit) and later a real capacity becomes known. Switching modes re-anchors the Reference from "hottest observed Cell" to a fixed Effective Capacity, so the same load can jump from a comfortable-looking 0.6 to a true 1.2 overload — or the reverse. The colours are only comparable within one mode's Reference.

Where it's a simplification / the danger of Additive: a relative scale has no notion of "safe". A quiet-but-fine period can look alarming simply because it contains the hottest Cell in view — the Normalisation Max is set by the data, not by any real limit. Re-render a calmer window and the same days recolour. Never read Additive red as "over capacity"; it only ever means "hottest here."

Why this is the first real decision

Every other choice in a Scenario — Capacity, Reducers, Consumers, the colour you end up staring at — hangs off this fork. Get it wrong and a heatmap is "pretty but wrong": Additive red mistaken for overload, or a real ceiling you forgot to declare so genuine overloads never show.

Pick the mode

For each situation, choose the mode that models it correctly.

A nurse can see 20 patients per day, and you want to flag the days that go over.

A social account with no hard limit; you just want to spot the busiest posting weeks.

A kitchen with a known ceiling of covers it can plate per day.

Website mentions of a brand over time, with no defined capacity — you want relative heat.

In the tool, the mode is a single Scenario setting. Choosing Subtractive reveals the Capacity, Base Capacity, and Reducer fields; choosing Additive hides them and offers a Normalisation Max instead. Everything else — Consumers, days, the ramp — stays identical.
