Capacity Modeling · Lesson 1

What is capacity modeling?

This workbench turns "how busy is something, over time?" into a picture you can read at a glance — for a person, a team, a company, or anything else with limits.

Imagine a row of little squares, one for each day. Each square is coloured by how busy that day is. Cool blue means quiet. Warm red means slammed. A whole page of coloured squares lets you see the busy times without reading a single number.

quietslammed

That's all this tool does: it paints busy-ness over time. You can point it at anything that fills up — a person's week, a kitchen at dinner, a team's month. If it can get full, you can colour it.

Blue = lots of room left. Red = almost full or too full. One square = one day.

A capacity model answers one question over time: how much of the available room is being used? The tool renders this as a heatmap where each Cell is one Subject on one day, painted by its Load Intensity — a single value on a blue→red ramp where 0 is empty and 1.0 is "full".

0.0 empty1.0+ full

The Subject is whatever you're modelling — it isn't a fixed type. Facet by person and each Subject is a person; don't facet and the Subject is the whole aggregate. Capacity and demand simply sum across whatever you're not splitting by. So the same machine models an individual, a team, or a business without a separate "hierarchy" feature.g

Formally, the tool is a normalisation engine over a time series. Every Cell reduces to one scalar, Load Intensity = load ÷ Reference, mapped onto a single perceptual colour ramp. The design bet is that a unified intensity — one number, one ramp — is worth more than domain-specific dashboards, because it makes heterogeneous subjects (people, pipelines, budgets, kitchens) directly comparable at a glance.

What varies between problems is only the Reference (what maps to full-red 1.0) and how load accumulates — captured by two Modes (Subtractive vs Additive, Lesson 3). Aggregation is deliberately just summation over any un-faceted Dimension ("rollup = summation"), which is correct for fungible sources and wrong for coupled ones — the constraint problem you'll meet in Lesson 7.g

The visual grammar — small coloured cells in a calendar, faceted into small multiples — is Tufte's argument that dense, comparable, well-designed graphics beat tables and prose for spotting pattern and exception.1 The modeling discipline underneath is classic operations management: capacity vs load, utilisation, and constraints.2

Where it's a simplification: collapsing a day to one scalar hides within-day timing and queueing dynamics. The heatmap tells you a day is hot; it doesn't tell you whether work queued or was dropped. Lesson 6 covers what that scalar can and can't say.

Why bother?

Because "we're busy" is not a plan. A capacity model turns a vague feeling into something you can point at: which weeks are overloaded, by how much, and whether adding help would even change it. The rest of this course teaches you to build models that are trustworthy — not just colourful.

Your turn — what could you model?

A heatmap Subject can be anything with room that fills up over time. For each candidate, decide: can this workbench model it?

A single engineer's weekly workload across a quarter.

A restaurant kitchen's covers served per day over a festive season.

The exact minute-by-minute order in which one person did tasks yesterday.

In the tool, this whole idea is one Scenario: a saved model (its Subject, capacity, demand, and config) that renders to a one-page visual. Everything you learn in this course is about filling in a Scenario well.
