Capacity Modeling · Lesson 7

Composition & constraints: do sources add up, or does the weakest link win?

The moment a Subject has more than one capacity source, you face one fork: do the sources sum, or is one of them a bottleneck? Getting it wrong makes the heatmap confidently lie.

There are two ways to combine sources of capacity.

Way one: they add up. If three people can each do the same job and any of them can take the next task, then three people get through three people's worth of work. Put them together and their capacities simply add.

Way two: the slowest one wins. If the work has to pass through several steps in order — step one, then step two, then step three — then the slowest step sets the pace for everything. A relay team is only as fast as its slowest runner; a chain is only as strong as its weakest link. Speeding up the fast runners doesn't help at all.

Interchangeable helpers = capacities add. A chain of required steps = the weakest link caps the whole thing.

You already model a single Subject's capacity well (Base Capacity, Reducers, one Declared Unit). When a Subject draws capacity from more than one Track — a person plus a CI pipeline, two coupled tracks, five departments — the Composition rule decides how those Tracks combine into Effective Capacity.g

Sum

C = c₁ + c₂ + …

Tracks are fungible / parallel — interchangeable, any can take the work. Three cross-trained people get through three people's worth. Capacities add.

Min (bottleneck)

C = min(c₁, c₂, …)

Tracks are coupled / serial — all required to produce output. The least-capacity Track is the Constraint and caps the whole. Improving a non-Constraint does nothing.

The one-question test: “If I add one more unit of this source, does total output rise?” If yes for every source → sum. If only one source moves the needle → that's the Constraint, use min, and adding anywhere else is wasted.

Worked example. One engineer can prepare 20 deployments a day; the CI pipeline behind them can only run 8 builds a day. Naively summing (20 + 8 = 28) is nonsense — they measure different steps of one pipeline. Real throughput is min(20, 8) = 8. Hire a second engineer and it's still 8: you added capacity to a non-Constraint. The CI pipeline is the Constraint, and only raising it raises output.

The min rule is Goldratt's Theory of Constraints (TOC): every system's output is limited by exactly one Constraint — “no chain is stronger than its weakest link.” The primary source is his 1984 business novel The Goal; the plant-tour chapters remain the clearest intuition for min-vs-sum.1

TOC prescribes the Five Focusing Steps for managing a Constraint:

  1. Identify the Constraint (the least-capacity Track under min).
  2. Exploit it — wring maximum output from it before spending anything.
  3. Subordinate everything else to it; don't run non-Constraints faster than it can absorb.
  4. Elevate the Constraint — add real capacity where it actually binds.
  5. Repeat: once relieved, the Constraint moves; go back to step 1.2

This exposes a real modelling hazard in the tool. Aggregation defaults to rollup = summation (Lesson 1) — correct only for fungible sources. Apply it to coupled Tracks and the heatmap silently overstates Effective Capacity: it shows 20 + 8 = 28 where reality is 8, painting a cool, comfortable Cell over a hard bottleneck. The default doesn't warn you; it just lies quietly.

Where it's a simplification: TOC assumes a single, stable Constraint, but a Track can be the Constraint only sometimes — a shifting bottleneck. On a low-demand week the person binds; on a heavy week CI binds. A single fixed min Composition can't express that the binding Track changes over time; you'd model the periods separately or watch which Track is lowest per bucket.

Why it bites: the composite case

Getting this fork wrong is the most expensive modelling mistake in the tool. Sum a pair of coupled Tracks and you'll invest in the wrong place, promise throughput you can't deliver, and paint a heatmap that looks healthy while the real bottleneck is on fire. Everything below is one decision, made per Subject: sum or min.
